I wanted a portfolio that actually felt like me. Not the consultant-in-Inter-Regular version, not the can-I-fit-six-motion-libraries-on-one-page version. Something with a bit of weather on it.

I really like trains. Always have. There's something about a line on a map. Fixed stops, fixed order, each one with its own little atmosphere. It maps almost perfectly onto how I want people to move through my work. So the site became a line. Each section is a station. You don't scroll a feed, you ride the carriage. Home is one stop, projects is another, the post you're reading right now is just further down the track.

The Japan-by-way-of-anime stuff is less of a system and more of a vibe. I grew up with Persona; I still think Persona 5's UI is the strongest argument anyone's ever made for putting actual personality into an interface. So the kanji on the rumors grid, the vertical accents, the occasional jolt of red. All little nods. The "Velvet Line" name is a Persona joke. If you caught it, hi.

I'm not a designer. I have taste, I think, but I can't reliably get the thing in my head onto the screen without help. Claude Code did the heavy lifting on the parts I'd normally spend three weekends bouncing off. The grid, the easing curves, the typography decisions where you stare at two options for an hour and can't tell which is better. I brought the metaphor and the references; the agent brought the patience.

The goal was never "serious design statement." The goal was: someone lands on the site, they should feel like they're somewhere, and within about three seconds they should know what kind of person built it. I think it gets there.

The line is open. Pick a station.
